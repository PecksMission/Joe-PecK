Why I'm Sharing This Journey

When I first thought about documenting my surgery journey online, I hesitated. This is deeply personal‚Äîwhy would I share something so vulnerable with the world? But then I remembered all the times I sat in doctor's offices wondering if anyone else understood what I was going through.

As a former medic and CVICU nurse, I've cared for countless post-surgical patients. I've monitored their vitals, managed their pain, and provided post-op care. But I've never been on the other side of it‚Äîuntil now.

Brain surgery isn't something you face lightly. It's not just a medical procedure; it's a journey that starts long before you enter the operating room and continues long after you leave. There's the diagnosis, the decision-making process, the insurance battles, the physical preparations, the emotional processing, and the constant undercurrent of fear mixed with hope.

I'm sharing this journey because there's a gap‚Äîa gap between what healthcare providers understand about the clinical aspects of care and what patients actually experience emotionally, spiritually, and practically. I've been on both sides now, and I want to help bridge that gap.

If my honesty helps one person facing surgery feel less alone, helps one caregiver understand their patient better, or helps one family member know how to support their loved one‚Äîthen sharing this vulnerability is worth it.

As Romans 8:28 reminds us, "And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose." I believe this journey, difficult as it is, has purpose‚Äîand sharing it is part of that purpose.

The Decision That Changed Everything

"You need surgery." Four words that completely shift your world. When my neurosurgeon said them, time seemed to slow down. I knew this was a possibility‚ÄîI'd done the research, I understood the progression of Chiari malformation‚Äîbut hearing it out loud made it real.

What struck me most was how different this felt from all the times I'd been part of similar conversations as a healthcare provider. When you're delivering the news, you're focused on being clear, compassionate, and thorough in explaining the medical rationale. But when you're receiving the news, your mind races in a thousand directions: Will I be okay? What are the risks? How long is recovery? What if something goes wrong?

I got a second opinion‚Äîsomething I always encouraged my patients to do, and now I understand why it's so important. It wasn't about doubting my doctor; it was about needing to hear it again, to ask the questions I forgot to ask the first time, to give myself time to process before making such a monumental decision.

Both neurosurgeons agreed: decompression surgery was necessary. My symptoms had progressed to the point where the risks of not having surgery outweighed the risks of having it. That's when I knew‚Äîit was time.

What No One Tells You About Pre-Surgery Life

The weeks leading up to surgery have their own unique challenges that I never fully appreciated until now. Everyone talks about the surgery itself and the recovery‚Äîbut the waiting period? That's its own journey.

There are the practical preparations: pre-op appointments, blood work, medical clearances, arranging time off work, coordinating help for recovery, organizing your home for post-surgery needs. Each task is a reminder of what's coming.

Then there's the insurance maze. As someone who's worked in healthcare, I thought I understood the system. But navigating it as a patient‚Äîfighting for approval, coordinating benefits, understanding costs‚Äîis exhausting in its own right.

But perhaps the hardest part is the emotional roller coaster. Some days I feel ready, confident even. Other days, the weight of what I'm facing feels overwhelming. And that's okay‚Äîboth responses are valid and part of the process.

I'm learning that vulnerability isn't weakness. Asking for help isn't failure. And being honest about fear doesn't mean lacking faith. It means being human.

Lessons from Both Sides of the Bed

Having been both a caregiver and now a patient preparing for major surgery, I'm noticing things I wish I'd understood better when I was on the other side:

  • The power of presence: You don't always need to say the right thing. Sometimes just being there is enough.
  • Small kindnesses matter: A text checking in, an offer to help with something specific, a prayer‚Äîthese aren't small things to someone facing surgery.
  • Let patients control what they can: So much is out of our control. When someone asks our preference about anything‚Äîeven something minor‚Äîhonor that.
  • Validate the emotional experience: Fear, anxiety, grief over lost abilities‚Äîthese aren't obstacles to overcome; they're normal responses that need space.
  • Hope and fear can coexist: I can be hopeful about surgery outcomes while also being scared. Both are true at the same time.

I'm documenting these insights not to criticize healthcare providers‚ÄîI know how hard the work is‚Äîbut to help bridge the understanding gap that I now see so clearly from both sides.
