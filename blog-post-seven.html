From The Field  ·  March 3, 2026

Here I Am.
Send Me.

By Peck  ·  Combat Medic  ·  CVICU  ·  Chiari Patient

7 min read

Follow Along

I'm shaking uncontrollably. I can't get up on my own. I have a walker to help me move. I just had brain surgery. And somehow, this is exactly where I was always supposed to end up.

I want to tell you about a young man named Lance Corporal Jared M. Schmitz.

He was a rifleman with 2nd Battalion, 1st Marines out of Camp Pendleton — from Wentzville, Missouri. Right outside St. Louis. He was killed in action on August 26, 2021, at Hamid Karzai International Airport in Kabul, Afghanistan, during the evacuation. He was twenty years old.

He was born on February 25, 2001. Twelve days after me.

I was still in combat medic training at Fort Sam Houston when his funeral procession came through. I watched it on my phone. My parents were among the St. Louis-area residents who lined the highway for him. I didn't get to be there. I watched on a four-inch screen from a barracks room.

And the only thing I wanted — the only thing I could think — was that I needed to go. I needed to go correct what had gone wrong over there. I felt it like a pull I couldn't explain.

Five Months Later

When I got to my unit about five months after that, we were on red alert for deployment. That means within 24 hours, we'd need to be packed, on a plane, and gone. And here's the honest truth: as much as I had wanted to go, as much as I felt called to it — I was scared. It's one thing to want to answer. It's another to hear the call come in.

So I started reading my Bible. I started going to church. And I found this verse.

"Then I heard the voice of the Lord saying, 'Whom shall I send? And who will go for us?' And I said, 'Here am I. Send me!'"

— Isaiah 6:8 (NIV)

That verse became my anchor. I put it on my arm. Not because it was easy — but because it was honest.

What's Actually Happening in Isaiah 6

Isaiah is given a throne room vision of God. The Lord is seated on a high and lofty throne. The seraphim cry "Holy, holy, holy." The doorposts shake. The room fills with smoke. And Isaiah, face-to-face with the holiness of God, is undone. He says: "Woe is me. I am a man of unclean lips." A coal from the altar touches his mouth. Atonement. Cleansing. And then — only then — God asks the question.

That order matters. It goes: revelation of God's holiness, conviction of sin, cleansing, and then commission. You don't volunteer to earn the cleansing. You volunteer because you've already been cleaned.

The Hebrew phrase translated "here I am" doesn't mean a casual "I'm around." It means: I'm fully present. I'm at your disposal. I'm ready for your command. I belong to you. It's the language of an oath.

Availability doesn't equal perceived ability. It comes after grace.

God doesn't say, "Whom shall I send — someone qualified? Someone holy? Someone brave?" He just asks. And Isaiah volunteers, knowing the mission will be hard. Knowing the people won't listen. Knowing it will involve rejection. That's not emotional enthusiasm. It's formed obedience.

Now I'm the Patient

Right now I have a litany of medications running through me. I can't walk to the bathroom without help. I'm doing a 10-day inpatient rehab program starting tomorrow. I've always been the provider — the one on the other side of the bed. And now I'm the patient.

I think about a line from Patch Adams a lot right now. Robin Williams says it plainly:

"You treat a disease, you win, you lose. You treat a person, I guarantee you, you'll win, no matter what the outcome."

— Robin Williams as Hunter "Patch" Adams (1998)

Last spring, I prayed a simple prayer. I asked God to give me the perspective of a patient. I didn't fully remember praying it until I was talking to my buddy Jesse a few weeks ago, and it hit me — I prayed for this. Not this specifically. But the perspective. And here I am.

I'm seeing firsthand what it means to be cared for. The vulnerability of needing someone to help you to the bathroom. The frustration when you're on enough medication that you can't properly explain what you need, and no one can quite understand. The way a nurse standing to the side of the bed — instead of in front of you — changes the whole feeling of a conversation. My dad always says: communication is 7% spoken words, 38% tone, and 55% body language. Being on this side of the equation makes that feel more true than it ever did when I was reciting it.

I was treating the disease. I wasn't treating the person. And I didn't even know I was losing.

The Thorn That Doesn't Go Away

There's another line from that film that I keep coming back to. Patch is standing in front of the medical board — the people who have the power to end his career. And he tells them:

"Now you have the ability to keep me from graduating. You can keep me from getting the title and the white coat. But you can't control my spirit, gentlemen. You can't keep me from learning, you can't keep me from studying. So you have a choice: you can have me as a professional colleague, passionate, or you can have me as an outspoken outsider, still adamant. Either way I'll probably still be viewed as a thorn. But I promise you one thing: I am a thorn that will not go away."

— Robin Williams as Hunter "Patch" Adams (1998)

The dean asks: "Is that all?" And Patch says: "I hope not, sir."

That's the same dedication we see in Isaiah. The same dedication I felt watching Jared Schmitz's funeral procession on my phone. The mission will be hard. The people will not listen. There will be rejection. I don't have a college degree. I'm lying in a hospital bed with a drain in my skull. But I know I'm being sent. I know I'm going.

Where I Think This Is Going

I believe God is directing me back into healthcare. I don't think He would put me in this position, give me this experience, let me feel what it means to be on the receiving end of a broken system — and then not use it. Especially given how few providers have actually been patients.

The blog is the first step. I want to expand this into something more — a space where patients can speak, where their experiences become feedback for the institutions that treat them. Not institutions looking downward, but patients looking upward. Maybe patient advocacy in the short term. Maybe something bigger eventually. I'll take it one day at a time.

That's what Isaiah 6:8 is. It's not a slogan. It's an oath.

I don't have all the gifts.
I'm currently sitting in a hospital bed.
But I know one thing —
Here I am. Send me.
