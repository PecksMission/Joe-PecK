From The Field  ·  March 3, 2026

It's Still Me.

By Peck  ·  Combat Medic  ·  CVICU  ·  Chiari Patient

5 min read  ·  Written from the hospital bed. Three days post-op.

Follow Along

This is the hardest post to write. Not because I don't have words — but because I've never been here before. I've always been the one getting up and going. Right now, I can't.

I can't get up and go to the bathroom by myself. I can't go on a run. I can't go to work. I can't work out. I have tremors. I'm shaking. Every time I cough, my head feels like a balloon filling with pressure. I have a walker to help me move down the hall.

I'm messed up right now. And I know that's temporary. But it's hard to sit in temporary when temporary is all you can see.

The Provider Becomes the Patient

I've always been the provider. Never the patient. That's not a complaint — it's just who I am. Me and my dad are cut from the same cloth: always doing something, always building, never sitting still. There are so many movies I've never seen because I couldn't bring myself to just sit down and watch one.

And now I've been on the same floor for five days.

Watching your mom cut up your food for you. Having to ask doctors to stand directly in front of you — not beside you — because you can't rotate your neck and you want to look them in the eyes when they talk to you. That matters to me. My dad always says communication is 7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language. I want the whole conversation. So I ask them to move.

These are the small indignities of recovery. Nobody warns you about them. I didn't know what they felt like until now. And I'm a medic. I've stood on the other side of that bed. I thought I understood. I didn't.

This is the training ground. The fire is hot. But I think it's supposed to be.

The Walker Is My Bench Press

I called an old buddy of mine — an old Marine. Old leather neck. Jar head. He told me my walker is my bench press and I need to treat it as such. That's my workout right now.

That wasn't what I wanted to hear. But it brought comfort, because it reframed the thing. I don't have control over my muscles recovering instantly. I have control over showing up to the work that's in front of me, gradually, slowly, every day.

I'm not going to be out of here tomorrow. Probably not next week. But I'll be out of here eventually. I won't run a mile tomorrow — but maybe I'll walk a mile in a month. I won't run a mile in a month — but maybe in three. I won't run a marathon in three months — but maybe in twelve.

It's all in due time. And the Lord's timing is always better than mine. Woe is me to think I've got a better plan than what He's working.

The Prayer I Keep Praying

An old friend told me once that she prayed for trials. Not meaningless ones — purposeful ones. That stuck with me.

One of my prayers has been: Lord, put me through the hottest fire to make me your strongest tool. So that I'm able to help others. It's a hard prayer to pray from a hospital bed. But I pray it. Make me into your strongest tool. Put me through the hottest fire.

I think this is part of the answer to that prayer. The fire is here. And I'm not going to waste it.

"Not only that, but we rejoice in our sufferings, knowing that suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope."

— Romans 5:3–4

What I Can Do From Here

This blog is the only thing I can do right now to carry my weight and love people well. So the blogs will keep coming. My thoughts will keep getting expressed. If that's all I've got from this bed, then that's what I'll use.

The Surgery Day page will keep getting built out as I feel more like myself and get more time near a computer. My mom and dad have been feeding updates to my buddy Jonah Sapp and my cousin David Peck — and those two have carried their weight for me in a way I won't forget. Every entry on that page, every timestamp, every update in the middle of the night — that was them. Thank you both.

A friend from church stopped by and said he'd never seen anything like this site — that it made him feel like he was right there. That's all I ever wanted. I don't know exactly what the Lord is doing with my life. But I know what He's done. And it's already something.

I have tremors.
I have a walker.
My mom is cutting up my food.
And I'm going to run a marathon.

It's still me.
